HISTORY

Palestine is a small region of land that has played a prominent role in the ancient and modern history of the Middle East.

The history of Palestine has been marked by frequent political conflict and violent land seizures because of its importance to several major world religions, and because Palestine sits at a valuable geographic crossroads between Africa and Asia.

Today, Arab people who call this territory home are known as Palestinians, and the people of Palestine have a strong desire to create a free and independent state in this contested region of the world.

The history of the Israeli–Palestinian conflict began with the establishment of the state of Israel in 1948.

This conflict came from the intercommunal violence in Mandatory Palestine between Israelis and Arabs from 1920 and erupted into full-scale hostilities in the 1947–48 civil war.
The conflict continues to the present day on various levels.

The Arab Muslims, Arab Christians, and Arab jews lived in harmony for many years, especially in Jerusalem.
The three abrahamic religions share many historic landmarks; even places of worship side by side demonstrating strong unity support, and respect amongst the groups.

After the holocaust, as the antisemitism grew in Europe, many Jews were kicked out making them refugees. Palestine took in most of the Jewish refugees.

However, as antisemitism got worse in Europe, the Zoinist movement bagan in the 19th century; an idealogy that wanted a Jewish only state.

To get rid of all the Jews from Europe - Britain stepped in and decided to give away Palestinian land to the European Jews without consulting with the United Nations, surrounding Arab countries, and Palestine themselves.

Eventually, temporary peace was made and land was divided.
However, the Zionists wanted more.

Britain and the UN stepped in once again and split the land to each group - howver the Zoinist land ended up getting all the agriculture, technology, transport, resources, etc. Whereas the Palestinians were left with nothing.

This soon got worse as Palestinians were denied resources andt their homes were illegally occupied and movement were restricted.

In 1948, David Ben-Gurion, then head of the World Zionist Organisation, announced the founding of the state of Israel in Palestine.

On November 2, 1917, the British foreign secretary, Lord Arthur Balfour, permanently changed the political and geographic landscape of the Middle East.

Outlining British support for the establishment of a Jewish state, it set in motion a series of events that culminated in the declaration of the previously been Palestine land - and the West Bank and Gaza strip.

Ultimately, the declaration promised a state to the Jews despite Palestinian natives comprimising some 90% of the population. In the words of Palestinian scholar Edward Said, this was a declaration "made by a European power ... about a non-European territory .. in a flat disregard of both the presence and wishes of the native majority resident in that territory". It's ramifications are still felt today.
